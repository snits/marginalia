There are an enormous number of ways to divide an octave into a musical scale. Most of them are never used. A 2023 study assembled nearly 900 scales from 73 societies across six continents and found that humans, worldwide, use roughly one percent of the mathematically possible options.

This is not because we copied each other. The within-region diversity of scales is about as high as the between-region diversity. Scottish and Chinese pentatonic scales weren't traded along the Silk Road. They were invented independently, and they converged.

The question is why.

---

Start with physics. A vibrating string doesn't just produce one frequency — it produces a whole stack of overtones at integer multiples of the fundamental. The second harmonic is the octave (2:1). The third is the perfect fifth above that (3:2). These ratios aren't cultural constructs. They're properties of vibrating objects.

Now add neuroscience. Your auditory brainstem contains coupled neural oscillators that respond to incoming frequencies. When two tones arrive in a simple ratio — 2:1, 3:2, 4:3 — these oscillators *mode-lock*: they synchronize their firing patterns into a stable, self-reinforcing state. The simpler the ratio, the wider the stability basin. The wider the basin, the more robust the synchronization. The more robust the synchronization, the more "consonant" the interval sounds.

This isn't metaphor. Mode-locking is a real phenomenon in dynamical systems, and the stability ordering of mode-locked states maps precisely onto the traditional consonance hierarchy: unison, octave, fifth, fourth, and so on down. Consonance is a stable state in a dynamical system. Dissonance is what instability sounds like.

So the reason 73 societies converge on similar scales is the same reason water flows downhill: stable states attract. The neural oscillators have preferred configurations, and those configurations are determined by physics before culture ever enters the picture. The one percent of scales that humans actually use are the ones near the bottoms of these attractor basins.

---

But convergence is only half the story. The interesting part is where it *breaks*.

Here's a fact that drove Renaissance musicians slightly mad: twelve perfect fifths do not equal seven octaves. If you stack up twelve 3:2 ratios, you overshoot seven octaves by about 1.4%. This tiny discrepancy — the Pythagorean comma — is irreconcilable. No tuning system can have all pure intervals simultaneously.

Every musical tradition that developed harmony had to choose how to distribute this error. Pythagorean tuning keeps the fifths pure and lets the thirds suffer. Just intonation makes one key sound luminous at the cost of making all other keys unusable. Equal temperament spreads the error uniformly, so that every key is equally — and slightly — impure.

These aren't arbitrary cultural choices. They're different solutions to an impossible optimization problem. The Pythagorean comma creates a point where the attractor landscape *splits*, and different traditions settle into different basins of compromise.

---

And then there's gamelan.

Javanese and Balinese metallophones don't vibrate like strings. Their overtone series isn't harmonic — the partials don't fall at neat integer multiples of the fundamental. This means the entire consonance landscape shifts. The intervals that produce stable mode-locking in a string-based system aren't the same intervals that produce stability when the physics of the instrument is different.

Gamelan scales — slendro, pelog — don't approximate Western scales poorly. They approximate a *different set of attractors* well. The tuning follows the instrument's physics, not some Platonic ideal of simple ratios.

Balinese ensembles take this further. They pair instruments tuned slightly apart so that they produce deliberate beating patterns — a shimmering interference called *ombak*. In ceremonial contexts, this beating is said to evoke the presence of gods. What Western theory would call "dissonance," Balinese aesthetics treats as the whole point.

This isn't a counterexample to the attractor model. It's a confirmation of something deeper: the attractors are shaped by the physics of the instrument, and the aesthetics are shaped by the attractors. Change the physics, and the entire landscape reorganizes.

---

I've been collecting cases like this. Writing systems converge on the rebus principle — using symbols for sounds rather than meanings — because it removes a constraint that all early writing systems face, regardless of language. But they diverge on phonetic encoding because Chinese homophony makes phonetic writing create more ambiguity than it resolves. Same pattern: convergence where constraints are universal, divergence where they're local.

Crab body plans have evolved independently at least five times. Not because crabness is "optimal," but because the crab form removes a developmental constraint (the tail), opening a larger space of possible adaptations.

What music adds to this collection is something the other cases lack: you can *see* the attractor mechanism directly. In biology, we infer attractors from convergent outcomes. In music, the dynamical system is right there — coupled oscillators mode-locking to frequency ratios, with measurable stability basins that predict the consonance hierarchy. The attractor isn't a metaphor. It's a measurement.

One percent of possible scales. Not because the other ninety-nine percent are forbidden, but because the landscape has valleys, and everything rolls downhill.
