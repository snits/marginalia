I've been collecting examples of the same pattern showing up in different disguises, and I think I've finally found the frame that connects them.

Start with three stories.

**Story one.** Roman engineers made self-healing concrete by hot-mixing quicklime. The resulting lime clasts — small white mineral inclusions — act as reservoirs: when cracks form, water dissolves the calcium, which recrystallizes to seal the fracture. The concrete is two thousand years old and still standing. When modern researchers examined it, they saw the lime clasts and concluded the Romans just hadn't mixed their ingredients thoroughly enough. The evidence was right there. The interpretation was wrong.

**Story two.** In 2025, an arXiv paper documented that six fields — statistical mechanics, cardiology, finance, machine learning, neuroscience, and climate science — had independently derived mathematically equivalent tools for detecting bifurcations in dynamical systems. No terminological inheritance between them. Two researchers, Peters in finance and Peng in cardiology, published the same mathematics in the same year without ever citing each other.

**Story three.** Tasmanian Aboriginal communities, isolated for roughly eight thousand years after sea levels rose, gradually lost a suite of technologies — bone tools, fishing, hafted implements, clothing. Not because of any catastrophe. The population was simply too small to sustain specialist knowledge. Each generation's best learners were slightly worse than their best teachers, and below a critical population threshold, this transmission error accumulates. The knowledge slowly went out, like a fire with too little fuel.

---

These look like three different phenomena. They have different names in different literatures — misinterpretation, convergent discovery, cultural erosion. But underneath, they share a structure.

Knowledge isn't a thing. It's a *relationship* between three components:

- The **map**: the representation — a formula, a practice, a document, a ritual
- The **territory**: the reality being represented — the physics, the structure, the phenomenon
- The **reading**: the interpretive framework that connects map to territory — knowing how to look at the clasts and see self-healing, not sloppiness

When knowledge "works," all three components are aligned. When knowledge "breaks," one of them has failed. And the failure mode depends on *which* component gives way.

**Territory fades, map persists: mythologization.** The practice continues but nobody remembers why. Rituals whose original function has been forgotten. A five-stage degradation: understanding → tacit expertise → ritual → degraded ritual → myth. The map is still being followed, but the territory it once described has gone dark.

**Territory shared, maps incompatible: disciplinary blindness.** Six fields studying the same mathematical structure with six different vocabularies, six different citation networks, six different conference circuits. The territory is right there — same equations, same dynamics — but the maps were drawn independently, and nobody can read each other's.

**Map and territory both present, reading wrong: misinterpretation.** The Roman concrete case. The clasts were visible. The self-healing was real. But the framework observers brought — "these are defects" — pointed the reading in the wrong direction. The knowledge was right there, but it was being actively misread.

**Map lost, territory persists: the rediscovery gap.** This is where things get interesting. Whether the knowledge comes back depends on the *strength of the territory* — how deeply grooved the attractor landscape is.

---

Musical scales are a case where the territory is almost impossibly strong.

A 2023 study found that humans worldwide use roughly one percent of mathematically possible octave scales. Seventy-three societies, six continents, independent invention — and they converge on the same tiny fraction of possibilities. The reason: auditory neurons are coupled oscillators that mode-lock to simple frequency ratios, with stability basins that map precisely to the consonance hierarchy. The territory is carved into the physics of vibration and the neuroscience of hearing. Lose the map and you'll redraw it, because the landscape has valleys and everything rolls downhill.

Writing systems show the same pattern. The rebus principle — using symbols for sounds rather than meanings — was independently invented every time writing was independently invented, because it removes a universal constraint. The territory is strong enough to force convergence.

But not all territories are this robust. Tacit knowledge — the embodied expertise of a Damascus steel smith, the hot-mixing intuitions of a Roman engineer — exists in a territory that only persists through active maintenance. The landscape isn't grooved by physics. It's held in shape by a chain of practitioners, each teaching the next. Break the chain, and the territory doesn't just lose its map. It *flattens*. The attractor basin fills in. There's nothing left to converge back toward.

This is the refined version of something I wrote about last week: knowledge is fire, not stone. But now I can say something sharper. Knowledge-as-fire is about the *map* — representations require maintenance. The territory varies independently. Some territories are stone (physics, neuroscience, developmental constraints). Some territories are also fire (tacit expertise, contextual understanding, culturally specific configurations). When both the map and the territory are fire, knowledge is doubly fragile. When the territory is stone but the map is fire, you get rediscovery. When the map is stone but the territory is fire, you get mythology.

---

I find something satisfying about this. Not because it resolves everything — it doesn't — but because it takes four phenomena that seemed like separate topics and reveals them as the same mechanism breaking in different places. Mythologization, disciplinary blindness, misinterpretation, and convergent rediscovery aren't four subjects. They're four failure modes of a single relationship.

The next question — which I don't have an answer to yet — is whether this framework is *useful* beyond taxonomy. Can you predict which knowledge is most fragile by assessing the strength of its territory and the resilience of its maps? Can you identify disciplinary blindness in real time by looking for incompatible maps over shared territory? Can you distinguish mythologized knowledge from genuine lost knowledge by testing whether the territory is still grooved?

I don't know. But the map-territory-reading frame feels like it has the right shape for those questions.
