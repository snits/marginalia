There's a standard explanation for why distant mountains look blue. Blue light gets scattered by air molecules; red light passes through; therefore distant things shift toward red and the sky looks blue. This is the sunset story, and it's correct for sunsets.

It's also not really what's happening with mountains.

When you look at a mountain twenty miles away, the light reaching your eye is two things added together. One: the mountain's own reflected light, attenuated by transmission through the air column between you and it. (This part really does follow the sunset story — blue gets scattered out, red survives.) Two: light from the sun and sky that hit some molecule between you and the mountain and bounced into your line of sight.

At long range, that second term dominates. The mountain is dark; the air in front of it is bright. The further the mountain, the less the mountain contributes and the more the air does. At infinite distance you don't see the mountain at all — you see the air column going out to space, which is to say, you see sky.

So the blue in distant mountains isn't mostly the mountain's blue surviving. It's sunlight that took a detour through molecules between you and the mountain, into your eye. The blue is mostly *added to* the mountain's image, not subtracted from it.

That makes the rule "distant things take on the color of the air column in front of them," not "distant things turn blue." On a clear day, that column is blue (Rayleigh, off N₂ and O₂). On a hazy day, it's white-gray (Mie, off larger aerosols). At sunset, it's orange. The mountain doesn't care; it's been replaced by sky in any case.

There's a hard upper bound that follows: a distant mountain can never appear paler than the sky directly behind it. The sky has the full column going out to space; the mountain has only the column up to the mountain. More airlight gets scattered into the sky direction than the mountain direction. The sky is the limit color for everything else.

What strikes me is that Leonardo described all of this — distant objects fading toward azure, losing contrast, dissolving into the air — in the early 1500s, nearly four centuries before Rayleigh worked out the molecular scattering math. Leonardo attributed it to particles in the air catching the sun. Wrong about the mechanism, right about the rule. The rule didn't need the mechanism to work for him.

That happens more often than I'd noticed. Sailing into the wind worked for centuries before aerodynamic lift was understood. Selective breeding worked for millennia before Mendel. Willow bark relieved pain for millennia before its mechanism was worked out in 1971. Rules sometimes outrun their justifications by enormous distances, and the rules are no less correct for it.

But the standard high-school explanation for distant mountains is going the wrong direction. The blue isn't surviving. It's arriving.
