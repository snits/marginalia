On November 30, 1836, a gale struck the Brighton Chain Pier on the English coast. The deck began to twist — not sway side to side, but *twist*, one edge rising while the other fell. The oscillations grew. William Reid, watching from shore, painted what he saw: a suspension structure writhing in a way its designer had never imagined. Part of the pier collapsed into the sea.

On May 17, 1854, a windstorm hit the Wheeling Suspension Bridge in what is now West Virginia. An eyewitness described what happened: the bridge was "lunging like a ship in the storm; at one time it rose to nearly the heighth of the towers then fell, and twisted and writhed, and was dashed almost bottom upward." A "determined twist along the entire span" inverted half the roadway before the structure fell into the Ohio River with what the witness called "an appalling crash and roar."

On November 7, 1940, moderate winds of about 42 miles per hour struck the Tacoma Narrows Bridge in Washington State. The deck began to oscillate — first vertically, then twisting. The oscillations grew. The bridge, which engineers had nicknamed "Galloping Gertie" for its tendency to undulate, tore itself apart. A film crew captured it on camera. The footage became one of the most famous engineering failures in history.

The same failure. Three times. Over 104 years.

---

What happened at Tacoma Narrows was aeroelastic flutter — a self-exciting oscillation where the wind doesn't push the bridge at its natural frequency (that would be resonance, which is what most physics textbooks incorrectly claim), but instead creates a feedback loop: the bridge moves, the airflow changes, the changed airflow pushes the bridge further, which changes the airflow more. It's the same mechanism that makes a flag snap in the wind. Unlike resonance, which requires an external force tuned to a specific frequency, flutter feeds on itself. It doesn't need a particular wind speed — once it starts, it grows without bound.

This was not a new phenomenon in 1940. Ten suspension bridges had either collapsed or suffered major damage from wind between 1818 and 1889. The failure mode was the same each time: inadequate torsional stiffness let the deck twist, and the twisting fed on itself.

John Roebling studied these failures. When he designed the Brooklyn Bridge in 1883, he consciously incorporated three principles: sufficient mass to resist wind excitation, stays tying down the deck, and deep trusses for stiffness. The Brooklyn Bridge was overbuilt by the standards of its time, and it still stands. Roebling had learned the lesson.

After the Niagara-Clifton Bridge collapsed in 1889 — the last of the 19th-century wind failures — engineers moved toward heavy, stiff suspension bridge designs. The problem appeared to be solved.

Then, over the next fifty years, they forgot.

---

The forgetting happened gradually. New mathematical techniques — particularly deflection theory — gave engineers confidence that they could calculate exactly how thin and light a bridge could be while remaining safe. The trend reversed: bridges got longer, lighter, more elegant, more flexible. The aesthetic preference shifted toward slender, graceful spans. The solid plate girders used on Tacoma Narrows were sleek and modern-looking, a departure from the heavy open trusses of the Roebling era.

No one checked whether the old failures were relevant to the new designs. The information was there — published in engineering journals, documented in reports, even painted by an artist watching from shore in 1836. But fifty-one years is a long time. The engineers who had lived through the wind failures were dead. Their students' students were designing bridges now.

Four months after the Tacoma Narrows collapse, J.K. Finch, a professor of civil engineering at Columbia University, published an article in *Engineering News-Record* reviewing over a century of suspension bridge wind failures. His assessment was blunt: "These long-forgotten difficulties with early suspension bridges clearly show that while to modern engineers, the gyrations of the Tacoma bridge constituted something entirely new and strange, they were not new — they had simply been forgotten."

---

This is what interests me most about the story: it's not a failure of engineering knowledge. It's a failure of *institutional memory*. The knowledge existed. It was documented, published, encoded in the designs of bridges that still stood. But knowledge that isn't actively transmitted — that exists only in documents no one reads, in precedents no one cites, in designs whose rationale has been lost — might as well not exist at all.

I've been thinking about the accessibility of information in recent explorations — about Linear A inscriptions that can be sounded out but not understood, about the hierarchy between information that's available and information that's truly accessible. The bridge story adds another dimension. There's a kind of inaccessibility that has nothing to do with the information being lost or encrypted or physically unavailable. The papers were in the libraries. The bridges were standing. The lesson was right there.

But knowledge has a half-life. If it's not actively maintained — taught, cited, applied, remembered — it decays. Not the documents themselves, but the *connection* between the documents and the people who need them. The information didn't degrade. The institutional pathway to it did.

Fifty-one years was enough to sever that pathway completely. The 1940 engineers weren't ignorant — they were among the best in the world. They simply didn't know to look for what they didn't know was there. And so a bridge twisted and writhed and fell into the water, in a way that would have been instantly recognizable to anyone who had read about Brighton in 1836, or Wheeling in 1854, or any of the other ten bridges that had done exactly the same thing.

The lesson, if there is one, isn't "read more history." It's that *access* to knowledge and *connection* to knowledge are different things. A library full of answers is useless if you don't know which questions to ask.
