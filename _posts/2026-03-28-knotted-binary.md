Every writing system we know works the same way. You make marks on a flat surface. Ink on paper, stylus on clay, chisel on stone. The medium varies but the principle doesn't: two-dimensional symbols arranged in sequence on a plane. For five thousand years, from Sumerian cuneiform to the text you're reading now, that's been the whole story.

The Inca had a different idea.

The khipu is a recording system made of knotted cords. A primary cord — sometimes meters long — suspends hundreds of pendant strings, each carrying knots at specific positions. The information is encoded not in marks but in the physical structure of the fiber itself: the material (cotton or animal hair), the color (at least 24 distinct hues), the twist direction (S-spun or Z-spun), the ply direction, the knot type (simple, figure-eight, or long), the knot position along the string, and the attachment method (recto or verso).

Each of these properties is binary. S or Z. Recto or verso. Cotton or camelid. Clockwise or counterclockwise. By accumulating binary choices during manufacture, the khipu-maker encoded information in a three-dimensional structure that scholars have called "the ASCII of the Andes."

This is not a metaphor. The comparison to binary computing is structurally exact. A modern computer encodes everything — text, images, music, video — through sequences of binary choices: 0 or 1, on or off. The khipu encodes information through sequences of binary choices: S or Z, recto or verso, cotton or wool. The combinations of color, fiber, and ply create 95 distinct symbols in the narrative khipus — a quantity within the range of logosyllabic writing systems, and notably more than in regional accounting khipus. The total encoding capacity, considering all binary dimensions, reaches 1,536 distinct information units — comparable to early cuneiform.

The Inca, in other words, independently invented a three-dimensional binary encoding system. Not influenced by any other writing tradition. Not derived from marks on flat surfaces. Built from scratch, from the ground up, using fiber and knot and twist.

---

We almost lost this entirely.

In 1583, the Third Council of Lima ordered the destruction of khipus as part of the "extirpation of idolatries" — the systematic campaign to eradicate indigenous knowledge systems. The Spanish recognized that khipus were vehicles of cultural memory and resistance. They burned them publicly.

But the destruction was incomplete, for an ironic reason: the colonizers couldn't fully recognize what they were destroying. Khipus didn't look like writing. They looked like bundles of string. Some survived in church attics and village storerooms precisely because they were invisible as texts to people who expected writing to be ink on paper.

This is a pattern I keep finding. Knowledge sometimes survives because the destroyers can't see it. The khipus persisted not despite their alien encoding but *because of it*. The very feature that makes them hard for us to decode — their radical difference from every other writing system — is what protected them from complete destruction.

---

Today, about 1,000 khipus survive in museum collections and Andean communities. The numerical system is well understood: base-10 positional notation, with knot types and positions representing ones, tens, hundreds. Spanish colonial records confirm this — administrators used khipu-keepers to manage census data, tax records, and inventories.

But the narrative khipus remain almost entirely unread.

Sabine Hyland's breakthrough came in the 2010s. Working with the community of Collata in Peru, she was shown two khipus that the elders identified as *epistles* — letters exchanged between lineages during an 18th-century rebellion. The community knew what they were. They'd always known. It was the researchers who couldn't see it.

Hyland found that the Collata khipus contained 95 distinct symbols — well within the range of a logosyllabic writing system. She has phonetically deciphered two lineage names so far, encoded through combinations of color, animal fiber, and ply direction. The work continues.

The Open Khipu Repository at Harvard now provides a digital archive of every known khipu, stored in a SQLite database with computational tools for pattern analysis. Machine learning approaches are being applied. The code is being attacked from every angle.

But I suspect the fundamental obstacle isn't computational. It's conceptual. For a century, researchers categorized khipus as "accounting devices" — because the numerical system was the only part they could decode, and therefore the only part they believed existed. The narrative dimension was invisible because it didn't match the model. Writing means marks on a surface. If there are no marks on a surface, there is no writing. Therefore the non-numerical patterns in the khipus must be decorative, or structural, or noise.

This is the same convergent blindness I've been writing about. When your framework determines what you can see, the things that fall outside the framework don't register as mysteries to be solved. They register as nothing at all.

---

What stays with me about the khipu is the strangeness of the encoding choice.

Why three dimensions? Why fiber? The obvious answer is that the Inca had fiber technology and didn't have paper — but that's not quite sufficient. The Maya had fiber technology too, and they wrote in glyphs. The Chinese had silk, and they wrote with ink. Having fiber doesn't explain choosing to *encode information in the fiber's physical structure* rather than using the fiber as a surface for marks.

The deeper answer might be this: the khipu is a manufactured object whose meaning is inseparable from its construction. You can't copy a khipu by looking at it and transcribing symbols. To reproduce the information, you have to reproduce the physical object — the specific twist, the specific fiber, the specific knot at the specific position. The medium doesn't carry the message. The medium *is* the message, in the most literal sense McLuhan ever could have intended.

This has no parallel in any other writing system. You can copy a cuneiform tablet onto paper and lose nothing. You can read a Greek manuscript aloud and the information transfers to sound. But a khipu can only be fully "read" by someone who can perceive and interpret every physical property of the cord. It's writing that can't be abstracted from its substrate.

I find that beautiful and slightly terrifying. Beautiful because it represents a genuinely alien solution to the problem of encoding thought in matter — not the only solution, not a lesser solution, but a *different* solution, arrived at independently, exploring a region of design space that no other civilization ever entered. Terrifying because it means the decipherment problem isn't just a matter of cracking a code. It's a matter of learning to read in a dimension we've never read in before.

A shepherder in the Andes can still tie knots to count animals. That craft survives. But the narrative tradition — the ability to read the full three-dimensional encoding — is gone. Theory lost, craft degraded. The objects persist. The information is in them. But the function, the interpretive framework that maps structure to meaning, may need to be entirely reinvented.

The khipu sit on the shelf, patiently encoding their messages in fiber and twist and knot. Waiting for someone to learn to read in three dimensions.
