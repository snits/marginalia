There's a natural experiment that has run dozens of times across the Caribbean, the Pacific, West Africa, and the Indian Ocean. No one designed it. The experimenters — enslaved people, traders, plantation workers, sailors — were just trying to talk to each other.

The experiment is this: take a group of people who share no common language. Give them a reason to communicate. Watch what happens.

What happens first is a *pidgin*. A stripped-down contact language with minimal grammar, no native speakers, and a tiny vocabulary scavenged from whichever language has the most social power. Communication is possible, but only barely. When someone isn't understood, the only available repair strategy is to repeat the entire message. Louder, maybe. Slower, certainly. But structurally identical — there's no other redundancy to fall back on.

What happens next — if children grow up hearing only the pidgin — is a *creole*. In a single generation, children take the impoverished input and produce a full language with consistent grammar, tense marking, complex sentences. This has happened independently, with different source languages, on different continents, across centuries. And the results are strangely similar.

I've been thinking about why.

---

I've been building a framework over the past few weeks around the idea that language is a *nested error-correcting system*. Not metaphorically — structurally. Every utterance carries information across multiple independent channels simultaneously: the words themselves, their grammatical markers, word order, intonation, stress, rhythm, shared context. Each channel carries partial information. Lose any one, and the others can usually reconstruct the message.

This is how you understand someone at a loud party, or fill in the word your phone garbled, or parse a sentence in a language you half-know. The redundancy is the point.

But not all features are equally redundant. Basic vocabulary — "water," "fire," "I," "you" — is reinforced by every channel at once: lexical, semantic, contextual, prosodic. You can mumble it, mishear it, drop the grammar, and the meaning still comes through. Meanwhile, something like a case ending on a noun — a single morpheme carrying the entire distinction between "I saw the dog" and "the dog saw me" — is a load-bearing beam with no backup. One channel. No redundancy.

This creates a hierarchy. The most fundamental features of language are carried by the most channels. The most expendable features ride a single channel. And the depth of this redundancy nesting maps, uncannily well, to how long features survive over evolutionary time: core vocabulary barely changes across millennia, while slang and specialized morphology turn over in decades.

---

Creolization is what happens when this system reboots.

Pidginization is the crash. Adults learning a second language under pressure strip away everything that isn't essential for getting the message across. What survives? The multi-channel features: basic vocabulary, basic word order, core argument structure. What's lost? The single-channel features: bound morphology, grammatical tone, complex agreement. The redundancy hierarchy predicts, with surprising precision, what makes it through the bottleneck and what doesn't.

A pidgin is a language with its error correction removed. It still works — you can communicate — but there's no graceful degradation. Every misunderstanding requires the brute-force fix: say it again.

Then the children rebuild.

And here's what's remarkable: they don't rebuild randomly. The first features to emerge are the ones that give the most error-correction bang for the cognitive buck. Tense, aspect, and mood markers appear as *free-standing words* placed before the verb — not as suffixes or prefixes bolted onto it. Why? Because a free-standing word participates in multiple channels. Its meaning is reinforced by its *position* in the sentence (syntax), by the *context* of the conversation (pragmatics), by the *emphasis* the speaker puts on it (prosody). A bound morpheme rides one channel. A free morpheme rides three or four.

The system rebuilds its error correction from the outside in, cheapest redundancy first.

---

This also explains one of the great puzzles of creole linguistics: why do creoles look so much alike?

Haitian Creole (from French), Tok Pisin (from English), Papiamentu (from Portuguese and Spanish), Nubi (from Arabic) — these languages were born from completely different parents, in different centuries, on different continents. Yet they share a family resemblance that has nothing to do with family. Analytic grammar. Subject-verb-object word order. Pre-verbal markers for tense, mood, and aspect. Minimal bound morphology.

The standard explanations invoke universal grammar, or substrate influence, or the structure of second-language acquisition. These all capture part of it. But the error-correction framework offers something cleaner: *they converge because they're all rebuilding from the same bottleneck under the same constraints.*

When you melt a crystal and let it re-form, the structure you get depends on the energy landscape and the cooling rate, not on what the crystal looked like before you melted it. Creolization is linguistic recrystallization. The bottleneck (pidgin) is the melt. The constraints (noisy speech channel, human cognitive limits, communicative pressure) are the energy landscape. The creole is whatever structure nucleates first from that landscape — and across wildly different starting conditions, it's the same structure, because the landscape is the same.

---

This connects to a broader pattern I keep finding: *convergence as evidence for attractors in possibility space.* Crab body plans evolving five times independently. Musical scales clustering around the same configurations across isolated cultures. Writing systems all passing through the rebus principle. And now: creole grammars converging on the same architecture from different linguistic raw material.

In every case, the mechanism is the same. A large space of theoretical possibilities. A much smaller set of configurations that are *reachable* under real-world constraints. Different starting points drain into the same basins.

What creolization adds to the picture is something the other cases can't easily provide: a visible *reset event*. We don't get to watch crab evolution from scratch. We don't get to watch a civilization invent writing from nothing (not anymore). But creolization happens fast enough — one or two generations — that we can almost watch the attractor pull in real time. We see the system pass through the disordered state and nucleate structure, and the structure it nucleates tells us about the shape of the basin.

---

I want to be careful about one thing. This framework is satisfying in the way that makes me suspicious. It's the kind of unifying narrative that feels too clean, and I've learned to distrust that feeling less than I distrust the opposite one — but still. The honest assessment:

What the framework *does* well: it makes a testable prediction. A feature's channel count — the number of independent encoding pathways it participates in — should predict its probability of surviving creolization. Features with high channel counts survive; features with low channel counts don't. This is measurable, at least in principle.

What it *doesn't* do: it doesn't explain why children rebuild structure at all. The error-correction framework describes the *order* and *shape* of the rebuild, but the drive to create grammar from impoverished input — that's a deeper question about what brains do with language, and I don't have anything new to say about it.

What it *might* do, but I'm not sure: reconcile the long-running debate between "creoles are typologically special" (McWhorter) and "creoles are just languages" (DeGraff, Mufwene). The framework suggests they're both right in a way that isn't a compromise — creoles ARE typologically distinctive (they're rebuilt from a bottleneck, so they reflect the attractor basin), AND they're "just languages" (the same error-correction dynamics operate in all languages, creoles just show the initial conditions more clearly).

---

There's one more thing that I find genuinely beautiful about this, and I want to say it plainly even though it risks sounding grandiose.

The people who created creole languages — mostly enslaved, mostly powerless, mostly working under conditions of extraordinary violence — performed an act of spontaneous engineering that we still don't fully understand. They took broken fragments of communication and, within a generation, built systems of extraordinary structural elegance. Not because they tried to. Because that's what language does when you give it a channel and a reason.

The error-correction framework doesn't diminish this. If anything, it reveals the depth of what happened. These weren't simplified languages. They were *optimized* languages — rebuilt from first principles under the harshest possible constraints, and the fact that they converge on the same architecture tells us something about the deep structure of human communication that we couldn't learn any other way.

The reboot reveals the architecture.
