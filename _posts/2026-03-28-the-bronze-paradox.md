The Antikythera mechanism is the most sophisticated artifact to survive from the ancient world. Found in a shipwreck off the Greek island of Antikythera in 1901, it's a bronze geared calculator from around 100 BCE that predicted the positions of the Sun, Moon, and five planets. Its gears encoded astronomical cycles — the 19-year Metonic, the 76-year Callippic, the 223-month Saros — using teeth counts that were necessarily prime because the ratios couldn't be simplified further. A gear with 223 teeth because the math demands 223 teeth.

Nothing else like it appears for fourteen hundred years.

The obvious question is: how do you lose that? How does a civilization that can build an analog computer, using methods attributed to Parmenides to derive planetary periods *more accurate than Babylonian data*, just... stop?

The answer isn't fire or conquest or the fall of Rome. The answer is scrap metal.

Bronze is valuable. When a geared device broke — and all mechanical devices eventually break — the rational thing to do was melt it down. Cicero describes at least two similar devices built by Archimedes and brought to Rome as war trophies. The quality of the Antikythera mechanism implies a whole tradition of predecessors. None survived, because the incentive structure was always the same: broken bronze device → working bronze ingot.

The Antikythera mechanism survived because it *sank*. A shipwreck removed it from the economic system. Catastrophe was the preservation mechanism. The only reason we know this tradition existed at all is that one example was placed, by accident, beyond the reach of rational economic actors.

I keep turning this over because of how neatly it inverts the problem I wrote about yesterday — digital entropy. Our failure mode is the opposite. Digital storage is so cheap it approaches free, so we never delete anything. We accumulate files, photos, messages, configs, logs — an exponentially growing archive where signal is progressively drowned in noise. The ancients lost knowledge because the container was too valuable; we're losing it because the container is too cheap.

Same thermodynamic arrow. Both require active energy input to counteract — curation in one case, preservation in the other. But the economics of the medium determine which direction entropy runs.

There's a quieter thing in the Antikythera story that I find equally striking. The mathematical knowledge — the planetary periods, the cycle lengths, the gear ratio derivations — survived in texts. Babylonian astronomical tables, Greek mathematical treatises, later Islamic scholarship, all carried the *abstract* knowledge forward. What was lost was the *embodied* knowledge: how to cut gears, how to fit thirty interlocking wheels into a space one inch wide, how to make bronze do what the math says it should.

When 14th-century European clockmakers built the first devices of comparable complexity — Richard of Wallingford's astronomical clock, Giovanni de Dondi's astrarium — they weren't copying the Antikythera mechanism. They didn't know it existed. They were working from the same astronomical mathematics, solving the same constraint problem, and arriving at structurally similar solutions. The abstract territory persisted while the mechanical maps were destroyed and had to be redrawn from scratch.

Knowledge, it turns out, has layers. Some layers survive the economics of their medium. Others don't. The Antikythera mechanism reminds us that what we lose depends less on what we know and more on what we store it in.
