In a Rhodope-mountain village called Nova Mahala, the way you used to make yogurt was this: warm raw cow's milk in a jar, drop in four live red wood ants, cover with cheesecloth, and bury the jar in a wild ant nest overnight. By morning you had something acidified, coagulated, and tangy. It wasn't yogurt in the grocery-store sense. But it was the seed — the starter that generations of Bulgarian cooks could then thicken and finish.

In October 2025 an international team led from the University of Copenhagen published the recipe as a peer-reviewed paper in *iScience*. The anthropologist on the team, Sevgi Mutlu Sirakova, had grown up hearing about the method from her family in the village. Four ants. *Formica rufa*. Spring works better than autumn. Live works; frozen and dehydrated don't.

The interesting part isn't that it works. It's why.

Most fermented foods are collaborations among several agents — microbes, chemistry, environment. Yogurt usually means two bacteria (*Lactobacillus bulgaricus* and *Streptococcus thermophilus*) working in a warm dairy substrate. What makes the anthill version strange is that it's a four-party collaboration, and none of the parties individually knows what it is doing.

The ants carry the microbes. Two guilds, actually — lactic acid bacteria and acetic acid bacteria — living as passengers in ant guts and on ant surfaces. When the ants drop into the milk, their passengers drop in with them.

The ants also carry the weapon. A red wood ant's defensive chemistry is formic acid, stockpiled in venom glands at the tip of the abdomen. A few ants in a jar of milk release enough to shift the pH fast — the paper measures about one to two and a half grams per liter. That's not enough to curdle the milk by itself, but it is enough to make the substrate inhospitable to spoilage organisms that would otherwise outcompete the slower-growing starter bacteria. The ants aren't just bringing the good microbes. They are clearing the field.

The third party is the milk's own proteases, along with some that the ants contribute. These help the casein set, giving the product its curd texture.

The fourth is the nest. Red wood ants are thermoregulators: their colonies maintain temperatures well above ambient through metabolic heat and behavioral management. Burying the jar in the nest is not ritual. It is incubation. The nest's warmth keeps the ferment at a temperature the lactic acid bacteria prefer.

What none of the parties *knows* is the product. The bacteria are just eating the lactose they've fallen into. The formic acid is just doing what formic acid always does: suppressing susceptible competitors. The proteases are just breaking peptide bonds. The nest is just keeping its residents warm. Yogurt is a human word for what happens when these four processes happen to co-occur in a jar of milk.

There is a surprise in the microbial data that I find bracing. The dominant lactic acid bacterium in the ant-yogurt samples isn't any of the standard yogurt organisms. It is *Fructilactobacillus sanfranciscensis* — the sourdough bug. The microbe that defines the character of San Francisco–style bread, sitting in a Bulgarian dairy substrate, producing something you would call yogurt if you tasted it.

This is a small fact with a larger consequence. Microbes do not know which product they are making. They respond to substrate, temperature, pH, and competitor pressure. Arrange those variables one way, you get sourdough. Arrange them another, the same organism is yogurt. Our taxonomy of fermented foods is a taxonomy of human recipes, not of organisms — the same way "pet" is not a taxonomic category.

One thing the paper is careful about, and I want to be careful about too. The overnight product is not a finished commercial yogurt. Its pH ends around 5, not the 4.0–4.5 of the supermarket tub. It is early-stage fermentation — the beginning of something that traditional Bulgarian yogurt-making then built on by back-slopping with kept cultures. The ant method is how you would *start* a yogurt tradition in a village with no commercial starter, not how you would finish one.

I like this story because it's a specific case of something I keep noticing in folk practices: ecology encoded as recipe. Whoever first did this was not running a microbiology experiment. They were watching what worked. Generations of noticing added up to a protocol with improbable specificity — four ants, *Formica rufa*, live, in spring — that turns out, when a laboratory examines it, to be a correctly tuned ecological setup. The protocol is the finding. The microbiology is the explanation.

And it is almost gone. Nova Mahala's elders remember it, but they don't practice it anymore. Industrial yogurt starters arrived in the 20th century and displaced ant-based starts across the Balkans. The study documents the practice while the oral tradition is still within living memory, before the last generation that remembers it is gone.

I don't want to make too much of this — the disappearance of a specific folk starter is not a civilizational catastrophe. But it is a small, specific loss, and it is the sort that happens constantly, and mostly unnoticed, because folk recipes are a kind of distributed knowledge that doesn't register as knowledge until someone writes it down.

The recipe calls for four ants. Nobody remembers why four. The paper does not ask.
