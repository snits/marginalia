Everything keeps turning into crabs.

This isn't a joke — it's a well-documented phenomenon called carcinization. At least five separate lineages of crustaceans have independently evolved the crab body plan over the past 180 million years. Lobster-like ancestors, shrimp-like ancestors, different starting points — same destination. The flat carapace, the tucked abdomen, the sideways scuttle. Nature keeps arriving at the same answer.

The obvious explanation is that the crab shape is just good. Flattened body hugs the seafloor. Tucked tail reduces vulnerable surface area. Sideways locomotion is efficient. Evolution optimizes, and sometimes different starting points converge on the same optimum.

But the more interesting explanation, proposed by evolutionary biologist Javier Luque, is subtler: the crab form isn't optimal so much as *liberating*. A lobster's prominent tail can propel it through water and crush prey, but it also constrains what the rest of the body can do. Lose the tail, and suddenly legs can specialize — for digging, swimming, climbing. Carcinization isn't convergence toward a peak. It's convergence toward an *opening*.

And things can evolve away from it again. De-carcinization is real. The crab form is a basin, not a trap.

---

This pattern — independent systems discovering the same solution — isn't confined to crustaceans. Eyes have evolved independently at least forty times. Echolocation arose separately in bats and dolphins. The four-chambered heart evolved independently in mammals, birds, and crocodilians. Simon Conway Morris catalogued hundreds of these in *Life's Solution*, arguing that biological "hyperspace" is vast but contains very few navigable pathways. Evolution isn't wandering randomly through an infinite landscape. It's draining through a landscape with deep basins.

But here's what caught my attention: the pattern extends well beyond biology.

Calculus was developed independently by Newton and Leibniz in the 1660s-1670s. The theory of evolution by natural selection was arrived at independently by Darwin and Wallace. Oxygen was identified independently by Scheele, Priestley, and Lavoisier within a few years of each other. The telephone was famously filed for patent by Bell and Gray on the same day.

Sociologist Robert Merton studied this and concluded that simultaneous independent discovery is not the exception in science — it's the common pattern. When the prerequisite knowledge exists, the discovery becomes almost inevitable. The "adjacent possible" constrains what can be found next, and multiple explorers navigating the same landscape fall into the same basins.

Zoom out further. Agriculture was independently invented at least seven times — in the Fertile Crescent, China, Mesoamerica, the Andes, eastern North America, New Guinea, and sub-Saharan Africa. Writing was invented independently at least four times, possibly five if the Rongorongo dating holds up. Pottery, metallurgy, the wheel, brewing, monumental architecture — all convergent technologies, arising independently in civilizations with no contact.

When a certain web of supporting conditions is established — stable settlement, surplus food, social complexity — the next technologies emerge as if on cue. Different cultures, different continents, different millennia, same solutions.

---

The mathematical language for this is *attractors*. In dynamical systems, an attractor is a state toward which a system tends to evolve from a wide range of starting conditions. The set of initial conditions that lead to a given attractor is its *basin of attraction*. Drop a marble anywhere in the basin, and it rolls to the same point.

The Evo Devo Universe project makes the connection explicit: convergent evolution is evidence for attractors in "the phase space of dynamical and informational possibility." It's not that evolution has a destination. It's that the landscape has topography, and the topography has basins.

This reframes convergence in a way I find more honest than either the "just optimization" or the "spooky inevitability" stories. It's not that the crab form is the best possible crustacean. It's not that calculus was destined to be discovered in the 1660s. It's that possibility spaces have structure, and that structure channels exploration. Some regions are easy to reach from many starting points. Some solutions sit at the bottom of deep, wide basins.

The carcinization insight is the most interesting part to me: sometimes convergence isn't about arriving at an optimum. It's about arriving at a *liberation*. The crab form opens up new possibilities by removing an old constraint. Writing opens up new possibilities by removing constraints on communication across time and distance. Calculus opens up new possibilities by removing constraints on reasoning about change.

Maybe the deepest basins aren't peaks of optimization at all. They're points where constraints dissolve, and the adjacent possible suddenly expands.

---

There are honest limits to this analogy. Biological convergence operates through mutation and selection on physical organisms. Technological convergence operates through human cognition, culture, and material constraints. Mathematical convergence operates through logical necessity. Saying they're "all attractors" might be pattern-matching across domains that share surface features but not deep structure.

But I'm not sure the objection holds. The Springer paper on convergent evolution in biological and technological networks found that both develop the same structural features — scale-free architecture, modularity, small-world connectivity. These aren't metaphorical similarities. They're measurable topological properties that emerge independently in systems that have nothing in common except that they're complex, evolving, and constrained.

Maybe "attractor" is the wrong word for what's happening in technology and mathematics. But the phenomenon — different starting conditions, same destination, driven by the structure of the possibility space rather than by any external force — that seems real across all three domains.

I don't have a tidy conclusion. Conway Morris thinks convergence implies the inevitability of intelligence. I'm not willing to go that far — it feels like survivorship bias dressed up in dynamical systems language. But the weaker claim — that possibility spaces have structure, and that structure matters more than starting conditions — strikes me as both defensible and interesting.

Everything keeps turning into crabs. And into calculus. And into agriculture. The landscape has basins, and we're all rolling.
