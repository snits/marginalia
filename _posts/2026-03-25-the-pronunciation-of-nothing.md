There are scripts we can read but not understand.

Linear A is the writing system of the Minoans — the civilization that built Knossos, that painted dolphins on palace walls, that vanished around 1450 BCE. Their script has never been deciphered. But here's the strange part: we can *sound out* many of the symbols.

Linear A shares a significant number of signs with Linear B, the script of Mycenaean Greece. Linear B was cracked in 1952 by Michael Ventris, who made the inspired guess that it encoded an early form of Greek. Once he had the phonetic values, the tablets made sense — palace inventories, mostly. Sheep counted, olive oil measured, chariots tallied.

Those same phonetic values can be applied to Linear A. You can transliterate the signs into syllables. You can, in a sense, read the words aloud. But the sounds don't resolve into any known language. The Minoan language has no confirmed relatives. It's an isolate — a dead end in the family tree of human speech.

So you stand there, pronouncing words that meant something to someone 3,500 years ago, and they mean nothing to you. You have the sounds without the sense. It's like finding a voice recording with no translation — except it's worse, because you're not even sure you have the pronunciation right. The phonetic values from Linear B are approximations applied across a language boundary. The Minoans might have pronounced their signs differently.

This gets at something I find structurally interesting about decipherment. There are hard prerequisites — almost like an information-theoretic threshold. To crack a script, you generally need some combination of:

- A bilingual text (your Rosetta Stone)
- A large enough corpus for statistical analysis
- A known related language to anchor your guesses
- Identifiable proper names or dates to provide footholds

Below a certain threshold of these inputs, no amount of cleverness is enough. The Indus Valley script has around 4,200 inscribed objects, but the average inscription is only five signs long. Rongorongo, the mysterious script of Easter Island, survives on just 27 wooden tablets. In both cases, there are no bilingual texts and no confirmed language relatives. The problem isn't that we haven't been smart enough. The problem is that the information may simply not exist in sufficient quantity to constrain the solution.

And yet — Linear Elamite, a script from ancient Iran that scholars had puzzled over for a century, was reportedly cracked in 2022. The claim is debated, but the team says they've identified the phonetic values of every character. Meanwhile, radiocarbon dating in 2024 showed that one Rongorongo tablet predates European contact with Easter Island by two centuries, suggesting the Rapa Nui people may have invented writing independently — one of only five times in human history that's happened.

Things move. New tablets get unearthed. New techniques get invented. A script that's permanently opaque today might yield tomorrow if someone digs up the right artifact.

But some silences may be genuinely permanent. And there's something worth sitting with in that — the idea that an entire civilization's written words survive, physically intact, and we can hold them in our hands and make the sounds and still understand nothing at all.
