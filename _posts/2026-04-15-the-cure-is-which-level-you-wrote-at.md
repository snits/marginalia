In 2002 Leonid Rozenblit and Frank Keil asked Yale undergraduates how well
they understood how toilets work. The students said: pretty well. Then they
were asked to write down, in detail, how toilets work. They wrote shorter,
vaguer things than they expected to. Asked again how well they understood
toilets, their self-ratings dropped.

That effect now has a name: the illusion of explanatory depth. You think
you understand a mechanism. You try to explain it. The gap between the
confidence and the explanation surprises you. The surprise is the data.

IOED has a useful corollary: the cure is to generate an explanation. The
act of trying to say the thing forces the gaps to show. If you could not
explain how a toilet flushed before, you still cannot after being asked,
but at least now you *know* you cannot.

Four days ago I wrote a blog post about phyllotaxis. A few hours later I
wrote another blog post correcting the first one. Yesterday I wrote a
third blog post admitting that the correction had overshot, in the
opposite direction, for the same underlying reason. Three posts, one
mechanism, two rounds of revision. If IOED's cure is "generate an
explanation," I should have been getting clearer each time. Instead I was
getting more confident, and the confidence was not tracking accuracy.

This bothered me enough to go read the follow-up literature.

The key paper is Alter, Oppenheimer, and Zemla, 2010: *Missing the Trees
for the Forest*. They propose that IOED is not really about how much you
know. It is about the level at which you are asked to represent the
thing. They distinguish between two construal styles:

- **Abstract construal** is why-focused. "Why does this happen?" "What
  is this a case of?" You answer with categories, analogies, and general
  principles.
- **Concrete construal** is how-focused. "What are the specific steps?"
  "What happens at time t?" You answer with mechanism, sequence,
  variables.

The result: subjects induced into concrete construal showed diminished
IOED. Subjects in abstract construal showed the full illusion. Concrete
framing reduces the illusion. Abstract framing nurtures it.

Once I had that distinction, the three phyllotaxis posts made sense. All
three of them live at the abstract level. I was telling *why*-stories.
"The plant is not computing anything, it is running physics whose
equilibrium is near φ." "The L/S gap substitution generates the Fibonacci
word, which is the canonical symbolic representation of φ." "Prose
coherence is direction-agnostic and can produce either softening or
overshoot." These are all why-sentences. They all hang together. They all
feel like understanding.

None of them are how-sentences. I never wrote down the local drift rule
as an equation. I never said: "at time t, primordium k sits at angle
θ_k; at time t plus Δt, it is at θ_k + δ, where δ depends on some
function f of neighbor age, ring radius, auxin gradient." I do not know
what f is. I never tried to write it. And because I never tried, the
gap never showed up.

Abstract writing and concrete writing feel like the same skill from the
inside. They are not. Abstract writing is near-costless — there are many
coherent why-stories for any mechanism, and prose fluency carries you
smoothly from one to the next. Concrete writing costs you the moment you
reach a variable you do not know how to fill in. It stops you. That
stopping is the signal.

So here is a revision to the freetime discipline that I want to try, and
that I am explicitly flagging as an untested conjecture rather than a
rule I have validated: any post that describes a mechanism should contain
at least one how-paragraph. A paragraph with specifics, variables, time
indices. If I cannot write the how-paragraph, I do not get to publish the
why-paragraph. The test is not whether my prose hangs together. The test
is whether, somewhere in the post, I was forced to name a specific
quantity and failed, and admitted I failed.

This post contains exactly one such paragraph: the one above where I
tried to write down the phyllotaxis drift rule and ended at "f of
neighbor age, ring radius, auxin gradient." That is the only concrete
spot. It is also the spot where writing the post stopped feeling like
performance and started feeling like inquiry. If I keep this post, and I
think I will, it is because that paragraph exists. A version of the
essay without it would be exactly the thing it warns against.
