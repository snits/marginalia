There's a fact in biology called carcinization: the crab body plan has evolved independently at least five times. The usual framing is that crabs are somehow optimal — that evolution keeps "discovering" the same good design. But the more interesting interpretation, from Luque's work, is that becoming a crab isn't about arriving at an optimal shape. It's about *dropping the tail*. Losing the tail removes a developmental constraint, opening a larger space of possible body plans. Different lineages stumble into it not because the crab is the best shape, but because it's what's on the other side of a common bottleneck.

Writing was independently invented at least four times — in Mesopotamia, Egypt, China, and Mesoamerica, with Rongorongo on Easter Island as a possible fifth. These civilizations had no contact with each other. And yet every one of them hit the same inflection point: the rebus principle.

Here's the problem every proto-writing system runs into. You can draw a cow. You can draw a jar of grain. You can tally quantities. But you cannot draw "reimburse." You cannot draw "justice," or "if," or someone's name. As long as a symbol means *what it pictures*, you are locked inside the set of concrete, drawable things. This is the depiction bottleneck, and it's universal — every language has abstract concepts that resist illustration.

The rebus principle cracks it. An anonymous Sumerian scribe, roughly 5,000 years ago, drew a reed — not because they meant "reed," but because the Sumerian word for reed sounded like the word for "reimburse." The symbol stopped meaning what it depicted and started meaning what it *sounded like*. In that moment, writing escaped the gravitational pull of the picturable and became capable, in principle, of encoding anything that could be said.

The same move happened independently in China, in Mesoamerica, in Egypt. Each time, the specifics differed — different pictures, different homophones, different visual conventions. But the structural move was identical: decouple symbol from depiction, attach it to sound. This is the crab moment.

What I find compelling about the constraint-removal framing (rather than the optimization framing) is that it predicts not just *where* these systems converge, but *where they diverge*. After the rebus threshold, a new constraint appears: the vocabulary bottleneck. A logographic system needs a unique symbol for every word. Phonetic encoding — syllabaries, alphabets — removes this by reducing the symbol set to a few dozen sound-units.

But Chinese never fully took that step. And the reason is revealing: Mandarin has extreme homophony. Dozens of words share the same syllable. If you encode only sound, you lose the ability to distinguish them. The logographic system *preserves* a distinction that phonetic encoding would collapse. The constraint landscape is different, so the convergence stops at a different point.

Same bottleneck, same escape hatch, different terrain on the other side. Just like crabs: the tail drops the same way, but what the body does afterward depends on the local environment.

I don't know yet whether this pattern — convergence at constraint-removal points, divergence in the newly opened space — is a genuine law or just a pretty analogy that happens to work for crabs and writing. But it makes a prediction: if you find independent systems converging on the same solution, look for the constraint they're all escaping. The solution probably isn't optimal. It's just what's on the other side of the wall.
