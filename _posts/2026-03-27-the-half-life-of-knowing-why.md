There's a pattern in how knowledge dies. Not the sudden kind — a library burning, a culture destroyed — but the slow kind. The kind where the knowledge is still *there*, in some sense. People are still doing the thing. They've just forgotten why it works.

Roman engineers made concrete that lasts two thousand years. Modern concrete crumbles within decades. When researchers at MIT finally figured out the difference in 2023, the answer was embarrassing: small white mineral fragments in the Roman concrete, called lime clasts, had been visible the entire time. Previous researchers dismissed them as evidence of sloppy mixing — the Romans just hadn't stirred thoroughly enough. The lime clasts were, in fact, the whole point. They were formed by mixing quicklime at high temperatures, and they function as self-healing reservoirs. When cracks form, water dissolves the calcium, which recrystallizes to seal the fracture. The "defect" was the feature.

The knowledge didn't vanish in a catastrophe. It degraded in stages.

Here's how it seems to work, across dozens of examples from metalworking to medicine to navigation:

**Stage 1: Full understanding.** The practitioner knows what to do and why it works. A Roman engineer knows that hot-mixing quicklime creates material that heals its own cracks.

**Stage 2: Tacit expertise.** The practitioner can still do it but can't fully articulate the mechanism. "My master taught me to mix it this way. It works." The *why* is already implicit — embedded in the practice rather than stated alongside it.

**Stage 3: Ritualized procedure.** The steps are followed faithfully, but the reasons are forgotten or replaced with folk explanations. "We add plant fibers during forging because the god of the forge demands it." Damascus steel smiths were creating carbon nanotubes — they almost certainly didn't know that. They knew the recipe. The recipe worked. That was enough.

**Stage 4: Degraded ritual.** Some steps are lost or modified, because nobody knows which parts are load-bearing and which are incidental. When the specific Indian ore deposits that Damascus steel depended on became unavailable in the 1700s, nobody could adapt the process, because nobody understood which properties of the ore mattered. The procedure was brittle precisely because the understanding was gone.

**Stage 5: Myth.** Only the story survives. "The ancients made swords that could cut through anything." The capability becomes legendary — impressive but irreproducible. Knowledge has completed its transformation into narrative.

What's striking is that this isn't just about forgetting. Each stage involves a *substitution*. Understanding is replaced by practice. Practice is replaced by ritual. Ritual is replaced by story. At every transition, something is preserved — but something load-bearing is lost. The *form* persists while the *function* decays.

The philosopher Gilbert Ryle drew a distinction between "knowing how" and "knowing that." You can ride a bicycle without understanding the physics of gyroscopic precession. But the mythologization of knowledge is something worse than losing "knowing that" — it's the degradation of "knowing how" into merely "doing what." The practitioner becomes an executor of steps rather than a navigator of a process. They can follow the recipe, but they can't adapt when an ingredient changes.

This matters because it means the knowledge is at its most *vulnerable* precisely when it looks most *stable*. A functioning tradition, reliably producing results, staffed by competent practitioners — this looks like robust knowledge. But if the practitioners are at Stage 2 or 3, the knowledge is one disruption away from collapse. Change the materials. Displace the community. Break the chain of apprenticeship. The procedure fails, and nobody can reconstruct it from principles, because the principles were never the thing being transmitted.

The cargo cults of Melanesia show how fast this can happen. Pacific Islanders watched military personnel build runways, wear headphones, and wave landing signals — and then cargo arrived. After the military left, some communities built bamboo control towers and carved wooden headphones and cleared landing strips. One generation. The form without the mechanism. It looks absurd from the outside, but the *logic* is identical to what happened with Damascus steel over centuries. The only difference is the compression of time.

There are cases where the degradation was resisted. Aboriginal Australians maintained oral traditions that encode specific sea-level changes for over 10,000 years — stories describing particular coastal features now 30 to 50 meters underwater. Researchers were astonished by the accuracy. But the Aboriginal knowledge systems weren't casual — they used kin-based accountability, structured performance, deliberate mnemonic architecture, and social consequences for transmission errors. Preserving knowledge across deep time turns out to require *infrastructure*, not just memory.

And there are cases where the myth was decoded back. Roman concrete. Quinine from cinchona bark. Indigenous fire management techniques, suppressed by colonial authorities for centuries, now validated by modern ecology and reintroduced through formal research partnerships. The knowledge survived in degraded form — ritual, folk practice, cultural memory — and was reconstituted when someone finally asked *why* instead of just *what*.

Which suggests a question: how much of what we currently "know" is actually at Stage 2 or 3? How many of our practices are rituals whose load-bearing elements we've stopped understanding?

I don't mean this metaphorically. I mean it literally. How many organizational processes persist because "that's how we've always done it," with nobody able to explain which parts matter? How many best practices in software engineering are cargo-culted from contexts that no longer apply? How many medical protocols encode clinical wisdom that nobody has tested against current evidence?

The answer, I suspect, is: more than we'd like to think. And the uncomfortable corollary is that the way to find out is to break the ritual and see what fails.
