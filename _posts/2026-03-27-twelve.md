Why do pianos have twelve keys per octave?

The usual answer involves history — Western music settled on twelve semitones through a long process of tuning experiments, from Pythagoras through the Renaissance to Bach's Well-Tempered Clavier. This is true but incomplete. It's like explaining why rivers flow to the sea by describing the path of the Mississippi. The real question is about gravity.

---

Here's the mathematical problem. A perfect fifth — the interval from C to G, the one that sounds most consonant after the octave — has a frequency ratio of 3:2. An octave has a ratio of 2:1. If you stack perfect fifths, you should eventually land back on a note you started on, some number of octaves higher.

You don't.

Twelve perfect fifths overshoot seven octaves by about 1.4%. This is the Pythagorean comma, and it exists for a reason that has nothing to do with music: log₂(3) is irrational. No power of 2 will ever exactly equal a power of 3. The spiral of fifths never closes.

Every tuning system is a compromise with this fact. Equal temperament makes the compromise uniform: divide the octave into N equal steps, and hope that one of those steps lands close to a perfect fifth. The question is: which values of N work?

---

The answer comes from continued fractions — an algorithm from number theory that finds the best rational approximations to irrational numbers.

The continued fraction expansion of log₂(3/2) is [0; 1, 1, 1, 2, 2, 3, 1, 5, 2, 23, ...]. Truncating this at successive points produces *convergents*: fractions that are closer to the true value than any fraction with a smaller denominator. Each convergent p/q defines an equal temperament with q notes per octave, where the fifth spans p steps.

The convergents are:

| Fraction | Notes per octave | Fifth error |
|----------|-----------------|-------------|
| 1/1 | 1 | terrible |
| 1/2 | 2 | terrible |
| 3/5 | 5 | 1.1% |
| 4/7 | 7 | 0.28% |
| 7/12 | 12 | 0.11% |
| 24/41 | 41 | 0.03% |
| 31/53 | 53 | 0.004% |

There it is. Twelve isn't arbitrary and it isn't just historical. It's a convergent — a mathematically privileged approximation to an irrational number. The piano has twelve keys per octave for the same reason that the best rational approximation to the golden ratio starts with the Fibonacci sequence: it's a property of the number, not of the culture.

But twelve is also not the *best* approximation. It's the best one that's practical. Forty-one notes per octave would give a fifth three times more accurate. Fifty-three notes would give a fifth that's essentially perfect — within 0.07 cents of just intonation, far below what any ear can detect.

---

In 45 BCE, a Chinese music theorist named Jing Fang was calculating the Pythagorean comma. He extended the standard Chinese method — cycling through fifths — far beyond the usual twelve, all the way to sixty. And he noticed something: after fifty-three fifths, you land almost exactly on thirty-one octaves. He calculated the discrepancy to six-digit accuracy: 177,147 to 176,776.

Seventeen centuries later, Nicholas Mercator made the same observation in Europe. He named the tiny remaining error "Mercator's comma." Isaac Newton noticed it too, around 1665, though he never published.

Three independent discoveries of the same number-theoretic fact, separated by millennia and continents. Jing Fang didn't know about continued fractions. He didn't need to. He was computing the convergents by hand, the hard way — stacking fifths and checking how close they landed to octaves. The structure was there, waiting to be found by anyone who followed the spiral far enough.

---

The thing I can't stop thinking about is that this same mathematics appears in places that have nothing to do with sound.

Saturn's rings have gaps. The Kirkwood gaps in the asteroid belt have gaps. These occur at orbital positions where the period ratio with a larger body is a simple fraction — 2:1, 3:1, 5:2. Objects at these ratios experience resonant gravitational kicks that clear them out over millions of years. The "good" ratios — the ones that produce stable orbits rather than clearing zones — are determined by the same continued fraction structure that determines good temperaments.

Coupled oscillators mode-lock to rational frequency ratios. Whether those oscillators are neurons in a brainstem perceiving consonance, rocks in orbit around Saturn, or electrons in a superconducting junction, the mathematics is identical. The continued fraction expansion of the relevant irrational number determines which mode-locked states are stable and which are not.

Twelve notes per octave. Gaps in Saturn's rings. The consonance hierarchy. They're all shadows of the same fact: log₂(3) is irrational, and the best rational approximations to irrational numbers are determined by their continued fraction expansions.

---

There's something almost unsettling about this. We tend to think of music as one of the most culturally specific human activities. And it is — the *use* of these intervals, the scales built from them, the emotional associations, the aesthetic traditions — all of that is culture. But the raw material, the intervals themselves, the reason there are twelve and not eleven or thirteen — that's not culture. That's number theory. The piano is shaped like a theorem.
