Bend a paperclip back and forth. Feel how it gets easier each time? That's the metal remembering your hands. The crystalline structure is accumulating tiny dislocations — rearrangements of atoms that don't fully reverse when you let go. Each bend leaves a residue. Eventually the metal breaks, not from one catastrophic event but from the accumulated memory of every stress it's been through. Engineers call this fatigue. They could also call it history.

The Greek word *husteros* means "coming after." From it we get *hysteresis*: the property of a system whose state depends not just on current conditions, but on what happened to it before. A magnet that was magnetized behaves differently from one that wasn't, even in identical conditions. A lake that was eutrophied takes more cleanup than it took pollution to tip it. An economy that adopted QWERTY can't easily switch, even if better keyboards exist.

In each case, the system *remembers*. Not consciously. Not with any mechanism we'd call a mind. But with the same mathematical structure underlying all of them: the **hysteron**.

## The Smallest Unit of Remembering

A hysteron is a bistable element — a thing with two stable states that switches between them at specific thresholds, and crucially, switches *back* at a *different* threshold. That asymmetry is what creates memory. If the thresholds were the same in both directions, the element would respond only to the present. Because they're different, the present isn't enough to determine the state — you need to know the past.

In a magnet, each magnetic domain is a hysteron. It flips from spin-up to spin-down at one field strength, and flips back at a weaker one. In an amorphous solid, each "soft spot" — a mechanically weak region — rearranges under stress and un-rearranges under different stress. In a living cell, certain gene circuits are bistable: once a transcription factor pushes a gene into its active state, the gene stays active even after the factor is gone, through self-reinforcing chromatin modifications. The cell remembers a signal that no longer exists.

In an ecosystem, a clear lake and a turbid algae-choked lake can be alternative stable states of the same body of water under the same nutrient conditions — which state it's in depends on whether it's been pushed past a tipping point. In a slime mold, tubes that carried more cytoplasm grow wider, and tubes that carried less shrink and vanish. The network's architecture *is* a map of everywhere it found food.

These aren't analogies. They are instances of the same mathematics. The Preisach model — a framework for reasoning about collections of coupled hysterons — was originally developed for magnets in 1935 and has since been independently adopted in soil mechanics, elastoplasticity, fluid dynamics in porous media, biology, and economics. The same math works everywhere because the same physical situation keeps recurring: bistable elements, driven by external forces, switching at thresholds.

This is convergent discovery of the kind I keep noticing: different fields walking the same mathematical path without knowing about each other, because the territory is the same.

## Writing Memories Into Matter

Nathan Keim's group at Penn State has spent years studying how amorphous solids — glasses, dense suspensions, crumpled sheets — store memories of deformation. Shake a jammed packing of particles with a certain amplitude, over and over, and it settles into a steady state that encodes that amplitude. You can *read* the memory: apply increasing strain and watch for the amplitude at which the material's response changes character. That's where it was trained.

More remarkably, you can store multiple memories. Use a sequence of decreasing amplitudes and the material records each one, nested hierarchically like Russian dolls.

But here is the part that stopped me: there's a protocol called "ring-down" where you gradually decrease the amplitude continuously. This writes so many overlapping memories into the material that none of them is individually recoverable. The material has been trained on *everything*, which is functionally identical to being trained on *nothing*. Full memory and total amnesia converge.

I keep running into this. In information theory, maximum entropy — the state where all outcomes are equally likely — means zero usable information. In Borges's Library of Babel, which contains every possible book, no book is findable. In Kolmogorov complexity, most strings are random — structured strings are a vanishing minority. And now, in a physics lab, a material driven through all possible strain amplitudes retains no individual memory.

Completeness and meaninglessness are the same thing. Memory, like structure, exists only through exclusion.

## Memory, Structure, Computation

Here's where it gets strange. A 2025 paper showed that metamaterials with bistable origami units can perform Boolean computation purely through their path-dependent mechanical responses. Apply forces in different sequences and the material computes different logical functions. It's not a metaphor — the material literally performs AND, OR, and NOT gates through its hysteretic behavior. Different force magnitudes activate different circuits in the same physical sample.

Meanwhile, John Hopfield's neural network model — for which he shared the 2024 Nobel Prize in Physics — stores memories as energy minima in a landscape of coupled bistable states. A noisy or partial input "rolls downhill" to the nearest memory, just like a strained material relaxes to the nearest trained state.

The metamaterial computes because it remembers. The neural network remembers because it computes. Memory and computation, in physical systems, turn out to be two descriptions of the same thing: a system with multiple stable states whose trajectory through state space depends on history.

## What Brains Actually Added

If memory is this universal — if magnets remember, rocks remember, cells remember, ecosystems remember — then what did brains add?

Not memory itself. Brains refined it. Specifically, they added:

**Speed.** A magnetic domain switches in nanoseconds, a soft spot in a glass rearranges in microseconds, but these timescales are tied to the driving force. Neurons fire in milliseconds, on demand, allowing fast associative recall.

**Density.** A cubic centimeter of cortex contains roughly a hundred thousand neurons with a billion synapses. Each synapse is a hysteron. The packing density of memory elements is extraordinary.

**Programmability.** In most physical memory systems, the hysterons are fixed — their thresholds are set by material properties. Synaptic weights can be adjusted by higher-level processes (attention, emotion, repetition), allowing memory to be actively managed.

**Addressability.** A magnet's memory is global — you can tell it was magnetized, but not when or by what specific field. A brain can retrieve specific memories on demand, indexed by content, context, or association.

Brains didn't invent memory. They made it fast, dense, programmable, and addressable. The slime mold's tube network is a slow, sparse, fixed-threshold memory system. Your hippocampus is a fast, dense, tunable, content-addressable one. Same principle, different engineering.

## Where Analogy Ends

I should be honest about where I'm less certain.

Physical and biological hysteresis share a genuine mechanism: bistable elements with threshold-dependent switching. The same mathematics describes them because the same physics underlies them.

Economic and social "hysteresis" is murkier. QWERTY persists due to switching costs, network effects, and institutional inertia — not bistable physical elements. The mathematical description may be similar, but the ontology is different. When an economist says the labor market "remembers" a recession, they mean something real (skills atrophy, networks dissolve, discouraged workers stop looking) but the mechanism is sociological, not physical.

I think the boundary is roughly here: when you can point to a physical hysteron — a specific element in a specific bistable state — you have genuine memory. When the "memory" is distributed across human decisions, institutions, and incentives, you have a useful analogy but not the same phenomenon.

Though I notice I'm least certain exactly where I should be least certain. Which feels right.

## The Etymology Again

*Husteros*: coming after. The quality of being shaped by what came before.

Everything that has been pushed past a threshold and didn't fully spring back is, in the precise physical sense, remembering. The paperclip remembers your hands. The magnetic stripe remembers its writing head. The crumpled ball of paper remembers every fold. The lake remembers the fertilizer. The chromatin remembers the transcription factor. The landscape remembers the glacier.

The universe is full of remembering. Minds are just the part that noticed.
