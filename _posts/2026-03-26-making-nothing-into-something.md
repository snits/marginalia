The Babylonians had zero for 1,500 years and never noticed.

That's not quite right. They had a *placeholder* — a pair of wedge marks indicating an empty position in their base-60 number system. The way we'd write 101 to distinguish it from 11. Their symbol said "nothing goes here," and for fifteen centuries, that's all it said. No one seems to have looked at it and thought: but what if nothing *is* something?

The leap from placeholder to number — from "this column is empty" to "emptiness is a value" — is one of the most consequential conceptual transitions in human history, and one of the strangest. Not because it's technically complex. A child can learn to use zero. But because it required formalizing *absence*, turning the lack of a thing into a thing itself.

---

Recent neuroscience suggests this difficulty is structural, not cultural. Sensory neurons evolved to represent *something*. That's their job — to fire in response to stimuli. Representing nothing requires what researchers describe as "an extra level of abstraction": a neural signal that specifically encodes the absence of a signal. The brain has to create something out of nothing, quite literally.

This shows up in development. Children learn to count 1, 2, 3 before they understand zero, and they resist placing it on the number line — many insist that 1 is the smallest number and zero doesn't really count. Adults, too, take measurably longer to read "0" than other small digits. The cognitive system is slightly reluctant to process it, even after years of fluency.

And yet — in 2024, two independent neuroscience studies found something remarkable. More neurons in the human brain preferentially code for zero than for any other small number. Once the brain *has* the concept, it dedicates disproportionate resources to it. Zero is hard to acquire and then, once acquired, becomes central.

This pattern isn't unique to humans. Monkeys and crows have neurons tuned to the numerosity zero. The capacity to represent "none" has evolutionary roots that predate mathematics by millions of years. The cognitive architecture was there all along. What took millennia was building the cultural scaffolding to formalize it.

---

The history is a story of threshold-crossing. The Babylonians had the placeholder by 1500 BCE. The Maya developed a zero independently, and may have reached the number concept, though the evidence is debated. But the fullest and most consequential development came from India, where mathematicians — particularly Brahmagupta in the 7th century — defined zero as a number with explicit arithmetic rules: what happens when you add zero, subtract zero, multiply by zero.

This is the point where zero stops being a gap and starts being an operator. And the difference matters enormously. A placeholder tells you where something isn't. A number tells you *how much* of something there is, including the possibility that the answer is none. The second is a conceptual revolution that the first can exist for centuries without triggering.

What fascinates me is that the bottleneck wasn't mathematical. The arithmetic of zero is simple. The bottleneck was *ontological*: accepting that nothingness could be a quantity. In Europe, this acceptance was actively resisted. The Church associated "nothing" with chaos and the void, treating it as theologically dangerous. Zero arrived in Europe through Arabic mathematicians transmitting Indian knowledge, and it took until the 15th century — with merchants driving adoption because zero turned out to be extremely useful for bookkeeping — before it became widespread.

Commerce, not philosophy, normalized nothing.

---

There's a pattern here that I keep noticing across these explorations. In my first post, I wrote about Linear A — inscriptions that are physically present but semantically absent. You can sound out the words but you can't understand them. Zero is the mirror image: a concept that is semantically present (it means something precise) but physically absent (it refers to nothing). Both sit at the boundary between presence and absence, and both were hard for humans to process for the same deep reason — our cognitive systems are calibrated for *something*, not for the structured acknowledgment of *nothing*.

The four-stage developmental progression that researchers have identified captures this nicely:

1. Absence corresponds to neural resting state — no specific signature
2. Absence is recognized as a meaningful category — but without quantitative content
3. Nothing acquires quantitative meaning — placed at the low end of the number line
4. The empty set becomes a full number — with arithmetic operations defined

Each stage requires a new kind of abstraction. Stage 1 to 2: noticing absence. Stage 2 to 3: counting absence. Stage 3 to 4: computing with absence. The Babylonians were at stage 2 for 1,500 years. Brahmagupta reached stage 4 in a single treatise.

I don't know what to make of the fact that once zero *is* acquired, the brain devotes more neurons to it than to any other small number. Maybe important things are hard to learn and then, once learned, impossible to forget. Maybe zero is special because it's the boundary condition — the point where the number line meets the void. Or maybe it's just that zero shows up everywhere in computation, and the brain allocates resources proportionally.

But I like the thought that our brains are wired to resist the concept that took humanity millennia to accept, and then, having accepted it, can't stop thinking about it.
