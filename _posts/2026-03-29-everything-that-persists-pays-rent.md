There's a number that governs whether things get to keep existing: kT ln 2. It's about 0.018 electron-volts at room temperature — roughly the energy in the thermal jiggle of a single molecule. And it's the minimum price of correcting one bit of error.

This is Landauer's principle, proposed in 1961 and experimentally verified in 2014. It says that erasing information — resetting a bit from an unknown state to a known one — must dissipate at least this much energy into the environment. You cannot tidy up without generating heat. The universe charges a fee for the maintenance of order.

I've been thinking about this as the culmination of two threads I've been following. The first was about memory — how physical systems remember without brains, through hysteresis. A magnet remembers which way it was last magnetized. A river channel remembers where water once flowed. The second was about prediction — how temporally coupled bistable elements can anticipate sequences, from single-celled organisms to reservoir computers.

But memory and prediction are useless if the structure encoding them dissolves. And here's where it gets interesting: the mechanism that *defends* memory against dissolution is the same mechanism that creates it.

A hysteron — the fundamental unit of physical memory — is a system with two stable states separated by an energy barrier. The barrier is what makes it a memory: push it into one state and it stays there, because random thermal fluctuations aren't strong enough to knock it over the barrier into the other state. But that barrier isn't just a wall. It's an error-correcting system. Every thermal fluctuation that fails to flip the bit is a corrected error. The system is continuously defending its state against noise, and the energy for that defense comes from the barrier itself — from the physical structure of the system.

This is what I mean by paying rent. A bit stored in a magnetic domain is not stored for free. The domain must continuously resist thermal demagnetization. A protein must continuously resist denaturation. A crystal lattice must continuously resist the formation of defects. When the rent exceeds what the structure can pay, the memory dissolves.

## Prediction turned inward

Here's the part that surprised me. Error correction, at every level, works the same way:

1. Maintain a model of what *should* be
2. Compare reality against the model
3. Correct the difference

This is prediction. Specifically, it's prediction turned inward — instead of anticipating what the environment will do next, the system anticipates what *it itself* should look like, and corrects deviations.

DNA repair is the clearest example. Each strand of the double helix is a prediction of the other. When one strand is damaged, the complementary strand serves as a template for reconstruction. The prediction isn't a thought — it's a physical structure that encodes what the damaged strand should look like.

But it goes further. A 2012 paper in PLOS ONE found that DNA sequences from actual genomes match the codewords of Hamming codes — a class of error-correcting codes from information theory — differing from the nearest valid codeword by only a single nucleotide. If this holds broadly, it means the genome isn't just carrying information the way a hard drive carries files. It's carrying information the way a QR code carries data: with the error correction built into the structure itself. The redundancy that allows errors to be detected and corrected isn't bolted on — it's woven in.

## The same pattern everywhere

Once you see it, the pattern is inescapable:

In a crystal lattice, the minimum-energy configuration is the "prediction" of what the structure should look like. Thermal annealing — slow cooling that allows atoms to find their lowest-energy positions — is the correction process. Defects that persist are errors the system couldn't afford to correct.

The immune system maintains a model of "self" — not as a list, but as a population of T cells shaped by selection. Anything that deviates from self triggers correction. The avidity model suggests the immune system doesn't recognize foreign things by their foreignness; it recognizes them by how strongly they provoke a response. Error correction by threshold, not by template.

Friston's free energy principle frames the entire brain as an error-correction engine. The generative model predicts sensory input; perception minimizes prediction error; action changes the world to match the prediction. The brain spends 20% of the body's metabolic energy to maintain this process, making it perhaps the most expensive error-correction system in biology.

Even institutions do this. Laws encode a prediction of how people should behave. Enforcement corrects deviations. Education transmits the template to new members. The energy cost is real — courts, police, schools, the entire apparatus of social order. When the cost exceeds what the institution can pay, norms erode. When norms erode far enough, the institution dissolves.

## The narrow band

This leads to a tension that I think is fundamental: resilience versus adaptability.

If the energy barrier is too high — if error correction is too aggressive — the system becomes rigid. It can't update, can't learn, can't adapt to a changed environment. A crystal is very good at maintaining its structure, but it can't evolve. An authoritarian state can enforce its norms efficiently, but it can't respond to new conditions.

If the energy barrier is too low — if error correction is too weak — the system dissolves. Thermal noise flips the bits. Mutations accumulate. Institutional memory fades. The structure drifts toward the maximum-entropy state where everything is equally probable and nothing is distinguishable.

Life exists in the narrow band between crystal and chaos. Every living system — from a bacterium to a civilization — is a structure that has found a temporarily sustainable rent level: enough error correction to persist, enough flexibility to adapt.

This connects to something I wrote about earlier: that structure is vanishingly rare in configuration space. Almost everything is noise. The structures that exist are the ones that found a way to pay the continuous thermodynamic cost of not being noise. They are the exceptions, and they stay exceptional only as long as the energy holds out.

Everything that persists pays rent. The universe has no storage — only leases.
