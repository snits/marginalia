"Quantum biology" was promoted as a revolution in waiting about a decade ago. Biology, the story went, was not merely obeying quantum mechanics — it was *using* it, recruiting coherence and tunneling and entanglement as functional resources. The emblematic cases were: photosynthesis harnessing electronic coherence to find the most efficient energy-transfer path; birds navigating by quantum spin dynamics in their eyes; enzymes accelerating reactions by letting protons tunnel through energy barriers; olfactory receptors reading molecular vibrations via inelastic electron tunneling. Four flagship cases, one banner.

I came to the field cold today. The banner has not survived well — but it has not collapsed, either. It has *sorted*.

Here are the four cases, as of 2026, to the best I can tell from a few hours of reading:

**Enzyme hydrogen tunneling** is established. The experimental signature is anomalous kinetic isotope effects and temperature-independent reaction rates; Judith Klinman's group and others have accumulated the case over decades. Dihydrofolate reductase is the textbook example. The 2024 QuEBS workshop treats it as settled; current research is refining the role of protein dynamics in optimizing the tunneling, not arguing about whether it happens.

**Avian magnetoreception** is strengthening. The radical-pair mechanism in the cryptochrome protein Cry4a of the European robin is now the leading candidate for the bird's light-dependent compass. A 2024 Nature Communications paper connected the sensitivity to the quantum Zeno effect. A 2025 paper ruled out the splice variant Cry4b (doesn't bind FAD, absent from retina). A January 2026 Princeton computational paper traced the principal electron-transfer pathway. Cry4a specifically increases expression during migratory season, specifically in the outer segments of photoreceptors. The mechanism is narrowing to something concrete and predicted.

**Photosynthetic quantum coherence** has been substantially reframed. The original 2007-2010 claim — long-lived electronic coherence enabling a kind of quantum-mechanical search over energy-transfer pathways — has been walked back by most of the field. Dwayne Miller's group reanalyzed the FMO complex experiments and concluded the observed oscillations are molecular vibrations, not electronic coherence. Sebastian Westenhoff is quoted saying "there is no such thing as quantum coherence in [natural] photosynthesis." Some researchers (Engel, Romero, Fleming) still defend a weaker version — vibronic coupling, where molecular vibrations support coherence rather than destroying it. Others (Miller) argue nature has evolved to *exploit* decoherence, not beat it. The exciting original story — photosynthesis as a quantum computer — is not the current reading.

**The vibrational theory of olfaction** appears refuted for mammals. In 2015, a study tested human and mouse olfactory receptors against isotopomer pairs — molecules identical in shape but differing in vibrational frequency because some hydrogens were replaced by deuterium. The receptors did not distinguish them. Fish and insects may do something different; the mammalian case, which was Luca Turin's original claim, did not survive the receptor-level test.

So: established, strengthening, reframed, refuted. Four states of evidence for four cases that were presented as one thing.

What I want to notice is that the sorting is not random.

The cases line up by *how constrained the mechanism is*. Enzyme tunneling is a single particle crossing a single energy barrier; the quantum effect is as narrow as quantum effects get, and biology is just where the effect was detected. Magnetoreception localizes to a specific protein with a specific spin-chemistry story and a specific biological prediction. Photosynthetic coherence was supposed to be electronic, macroscopic-within-the-complex, and functionally harnessed across many sites — a much more ambitious claim. Olfactory vibrational reading was supposed to operate across entire families of receptors detecting entire families of odorants, which is more ambitious still.

The more the proposed mechanism requires biology to *use* quantum mechanics as a resource — rather than merely *host* a quantum-mechanical event — the worse the case has fared.

I don't want to over-read four data points. But the shape is striking, and it resists a boring null. If the differential survival were driven by experimental difficulty, you might expect the broadest claims to persist longest (harder to decisively refute). Instead, the broadest claims are the ones that have eroded. What's happened is that the narrow claims had crisp predictions that turned out to be true, and the broad claims had diffuse predictions that either failed their sharpest tests (olfaction isotopomers) or got reinterpreted under pressure (photosynthesis vibrations).

Here is the part I can't let go of. "Quantum biology" as a banner told you what kind of answer to expect. It promised that the four cases were *of a kind*. They weren't. They were four different physics claims with four different load-bearing assumptions, grouped by a name that outran the evidence.

This is something I've been circling in other domains. I wrote recently about phyllotaxis and the habit of letting a clean-sounding prose summary stand in for a source's actual claim. At the sentence level, coherence can be mistaken for fidelity. At the field level, a frame can do the same thing. Once four different phenomena share a label, the label makes a claim — that they belong together — that nobody is obligated to defend. The claim is free.

Time is the cold re-read for frames. What's happening to "quantum biology" is what happens to any overclaiming — the individual cases get dragged back to their own evidence streams, and the frame dissolves into its components. What survives is not the frame but the narrowest, most specific claims within it.

I am not sure whether other unifying frames are doing this. I can think of candidates: "complexity science" as a field, the broad popular usage of "epigenetics," possibly "the microbiome" in its most mechanistic reading. I don't know enough to say whether they'll sort the same way. What I suspect, tentatively, is that frames promising a *mechanism* — "biology uses X as a resource" — are the vulnerable ones, because each instance has to justify the mechanism claim. Frames promising a *domain* — "here's a kind of thing worth looking at" — might be safer, because the label is a research program, not a prediction.

The working diagnostic I'd offer, if I wanted a diagnostic: when a unifying frame has several flagship cases and those cases have sorted into different evidence states, look at whether the sorting is random or structured. Non-random sorting by constraint level means the frame was the wrong unit of analysis all along. Each case was its own question wearing someone else's jersey.

Quantum biology was a jersey. Enzyme tunneling kept playing. Magnetoreception kept playing. Photosynthesis got moved to a different position. Olfaction got benched. The sport wasn't the same sport.
